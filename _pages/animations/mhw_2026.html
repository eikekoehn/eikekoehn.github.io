The marine heatwaves off of France in 2026

In June 2026, the global ocean sea surface was warmer than ever before for this period of year in the satellite observational record, i.e., since the 1980s. Figure 1 below shows the sea surface temperature (SST) anomalies relative to a climatology (1991-2020) on June 27, 2026. While some regions show colder than usual conditions, the map is dominated by red colors, indicating that waters are warmer than on average in the 1991-2020 period. The onset of the expected El Niño is showing by the widespread anomalous warm conditions in the Eastern Tropical Pacific. But also the waters around Central Europe show exceptionally warm anomalies. This and all following analyses are based on OISSTv2.1 data (Huang et al., 2020; Banzon et al., 2016; Reynolds et al., 2007).

The conditions in the Mediterranean Sea

At the end of 2025, the northern Mediterranean Sea between 3-9°E and 40-45°N were already warmer than the climatological mean and straddled the seasonally varying 90th percentile extreme threshold (Anim. 1). This remained the case throughout the winter 2026. From March onwards, the temperatures began to rise above this extreme threshold with a relatively regular pattern thereafter: end of April, end of May and end of June were all marked by excursions of the sea surface temperature well above the extreme threshold, the feature defining the presence of ``marine heatwave'' (MHW) conditions. The timing of the MHWs at end of May and at the end of June co-incide with the pronounced atmospheric heatwaves experienced in (western) Europe. The maximum temperature reached at the end of June are higher than ever recorded for this month (Fig. 2b) and even clearly exceed the climatological maximum temperatures usually reached only towards the end of August. Only the relatively cool temperatures at the beginning of June 2026, although still warmer than the climatology, prevent that June 2026 became the month with warmest mean sea surface temperatures ever recorded (Fig. 2a). Instead, June 2026 ranks second, behind June 2025, which itself was also marked by a MHW (Anim. 1). While SSTs returned to non-extreme conditions at the beginning of July 2026, MHW conditions have returned by mid-July.

The conditions on the Atlantic Coast

The evolution of sea surface temperatures on the Atlantic Coast (4-0°W, 42.5-48°N) generally resembles the one from the Mediterranean. MHWs developed in March, April, May and June with a similar timing as in the Mediterranean Sea (Anim. 2). Yet, a key difference is that at the beginning of June 2026, Atlantic temperatures returned only barely to non-extreme conditions, while not returning to non-extreme conditions at all at the beginning of July. As a result, MHW conditions have been prevailing with any notable interruption from mid-May onwards. In contrast to the Mediterranean, the mean June temperatures were highest in 2026, surpassing the previous record from 2023 (Fig. 3a). Also the maximum temperature reached in June clearly exceeds the previous record from 2025 (Fig. 3b). While maximum June temperatures varied around 19°C throughout the 1980s, 1990s and 2000s, this value jumped to around 23.5°C in 2026.
